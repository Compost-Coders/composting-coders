    Kuituhamppu käyttöön -project introduction

    Kuituhamppu käyttöön is societal project falling into the 17 Sustainable Development Goals of the United Nations. The project categories are goal number 7; affordable and clean energy for all and number 13; climate action (Lapland UAS, 2026). Kuituhamppu käyttöön project is budget is 846 817€ and it is funded by the European Union by 70% and Lapin Liitto covers the rest 30%. Project is scheduled to take place between 1.12.2023 and 28.2.2026 (Frostbit, 2026).

    The aim of the Kuituhamppu project is to produce information on the potential of hemp, reed manna grass, and other similar plants as industrial raw materials to replace peat. The project examines the suitability of hemp and other organic by-products, among other things, as raw materials for energy pellets, insulation materials in construction, and growth substrates – without forgetting the economic viability of production (Frostbit, 2026).

    According to Sitra approximately half of the global peat energy usage takes place in Finland where peat has played a significant role in district heating, although its energy use has decreased clearly from its peak levels in the 2000s. Peat is used both for energy production and for other purposes such as growing media and animal bedding. Globally, energy use of peat is declining, while its non-energy use may increase as there is global demand particularly in China (Soimakallio et al. 2020).

    In 2020, the Finnish government decided to halve the use of peat for energy, after which its use declined rapidly. The goal has been to cut peat energy use by 50% by 2030. However, the war in Ukraine is changing the direction of energy policy, as Finland has considered restoring peat production to replace some of the energy means imported from Russia (Fritze, 2022). However, according to Neova Group, which is one of the largest peat manufacturers in Finland, peat use in Finland has decreased to about one-third of its 2019 level compared to 2025due to electrification, but it remains important for energy security, while mild weather in the Nordics has reduced heating demand and caused excess biomass supply (Neova, p. 64, 2025).

    The use of peat for energy has significant negative impacts on the climate and environment: it produces high greenhouse gas emissions, pollutes waterways, and threatens endangered peatland ecosystems. Peat is not a renewable energy source, despite some claims, because it regenerates extremely slowly. Both the EU and the IPCC classify peat alongside fossil fuels due to its emissions (Fritze, 2022).

    Compost coders project focused on Kuituhamppu -project work package number 2 where hemp was investigated as growing medium in composts. The mean was surplus hemp that does not go to building materials and it was used in this case for composts which were in backyard of Lapland University of applied school building. The composts had sensors to read for example moisture and heat levels in the composts.
