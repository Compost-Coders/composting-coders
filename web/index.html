Project Aims

Research focus

The project investigates the potential of hemp, reed manna grass, and similar plants as industrial raw materials with the potential to replace peat across multiple applications.

Applications studied

Suitability as raw material for energy pellets, insulation in construction, and growth substrates, alongside analysis of economic viability of production.

Our contribution

The Compost Coders project focused on Work Package 2, where surplus hemp not used in construction was tested as a growing medium in outdoor composters at Lapland UAS.

Sensor data

The composters were equipped with sensors tracking moisture and temperature at multiple internal positions, generating the dataset analysed in this dashboard.

Peat in Finland - Why It Matters

UN SDGs

The project addresses Goal 7 (affordable and clean energy for all) and Goal 13 (climate action), both of which are relevant to reducing dependence on peat as an energy source.

Global context

Finland accounts for roughly half of global peat energy use. Globally, energy use of peat is declining, while non-energy use may increase due to demand particularly from China. Peat also remains significant domestically for growing media and animal bedding.

Policy shifts

In 2020 the Finnish government set a target to cut peat energy use by 50% by 2030. The war in Ukraine complicated this, as Finland considered restoring peat production to replace energy previously imported from Russia. According to Neova Group's 2025 annual report, peat use in Finland has decreased to about one-third of its 2019 level by 2025, driven by electrification, mild Nordic winters, and excess biomass supply.

Environmental impact

Peat combustion generates high greenhouse gas emissions, pollutes waterways, and threatens peatland ecosystems. The EU and IPCC classify peat alongside fossil fuels as it regenerates so slowly it is not considered renewable.

Key Findings
Temperature

Both composters reached thermophilic temperatures

In the actual dataset, the middle layer peaked at 59.9 C in Composter 1 and 60.4 C in Composter 2.

Moisture

Lower layers retained the most water

The lower layer reached 100% moisture in both composters at some point, while upper layers were often extremely dry.

Heating

Heating became most important during winter

In this notebook's filtered May to July slice, heating wattage stayed at 0 W throughout the exported rows.

Outside Conditions

Outdoor temperatures dropped far below compost temperatures

Within the exported notebook slice, outside temperatures ranged from 2.8 C to 26.7 C while compost temperatures climbed much higher.
